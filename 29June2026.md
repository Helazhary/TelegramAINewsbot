# Tech & AI Daily Briefing — 29 June 2026

The single biggest story today is **South Korea's $518 billion (800 trillion won) national push** to build out **AI chip and data-center capacity** through **Samsung** and **SK Hynix** — one of the largest industrial commitments in tech history, and the clearest sign yet of how compute- and memory-constrained the global AI buildout has become. ## 1. South Korea commits $518B to AI chips, data centers, and robotics via Samsung and SK Hynix

President **Lee Jae Myung** unveiled an **800 trillion won (~$518 billion)** national plan built on a "triple axis" of semiconductors, physical AI, and data centers. **Samsung Electronics** (under chairman **Lee Jae-yong**) and **SK Hynix** — which together produce roughly two-thirds of the world's memory chips — will each build two new fabs at a new manufacturing hub in southwestern South Korea (centered on Gwangju), expanding beyond their existing Gyeonggi Province complexes; Samsung also plans to double its **DRAM** output within five years. The plan is explicitly framed as a response to AI-driven demand for **HBM (high-bandwidth memory)**, the chips that feed Nvidia and other AI accelerators.

- **Why this matters for you (developer/entrepreneur):** Memory and HBM capacity — not just GPUs — is the real bottleneck behind today's AI compute shortages and pricing; a supply increase 2–3 years out should eventually ease the hardware cost pressure on training and inference.
- **Business/project idea:** Build a **supply-chain intelligence dashboard** for AI infra buyers that tracks fab announcements and HBM capacity additions across Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron, and TSMC, helping startups and cloud buyers time GPU/memory purchases and contract negotiations against future supply unlocks.

## 2. Google rations Meta's Gemini access amid compute crunch; pays SpaceX $920M/month for 110,000 GPUs

The **Financial Times**, confirmed by CNBC and others, reported that **Google** has restricted **Meta's** access to its **Gemini** models since around March 2026 because it could not supply the computing capacity Meta requested — delaying some of Meta's internal AI projects, which used Gemini for coding, ads, customer service, and content moderation. In parallel, Google has agreed to pay **SpaceX** roughly **$920 million per month** for access to **110,000 Nvidia GPUs**, part of a planned **$180–190 billion** 2026 infrastructure budget, while Meta accelerates its own internal model effort (reassigning thousands of staff, launching "Muse Spark" under Superintelligence Labs) to cut reliance on external frontier models.

- **Why this matters for you (developer/entrepreneur):** If your product roadmap depends on a single frontier-model vendor for production workloads, capacity — not just price — is now a real risk; even Meta got rationed. Multi-vendor, model-agnostic architecture is becoming a necessity, not just a hedge.
- **Business/project idea:** Build a **multi-provider LLM routing/failover layer** that automatically shifts traffic between Gemini, Claude, GPT, and self-hosted open-weight models based on real-time capacity, latency, and cost — aimed at mid-size companies that can't negotiate the capacity guarantees a Meta or Google can.
- Sources: [CNBC (citing FT)](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/06/28/google-limits-metas-use-of-its-gemini-ai-models-ft-reports.html) · [Data Center Dynamics](https://www.datacenterdynamics.com/en/news/google-limits-metas-ai-use-due-to-capacity-constraints-report/) · [Tech Brew](https://www.techbrew.com/stories/google-gemini-cap-limit-meta-ai-compute)

## 3. OpenAI previews GPT-5.6 family — Sol, Terra, and Luna — in gated rollout to ~20 partners

**OpenAI** began a limited preview of **GPT-5.6**: **Sol** (flagship, for hard coding and security-research problems), **Terra** (balanced, for high-volume business tasks), and **Luna** (fastest and cheapest, for everyday drafting/summarization). The models are available via the API and **Codex** to roughly **20 trusted partner organizations**, after OpenAI shared the models and release plans with the **U.S. government** first. Pricing per 1M tokens: Sol $5 input / $30 output, Terra $2.50 / $15, Luna $1 / $6. OpenAI says general availability in ChatGPT, Codex, and the API is planned "in the coming weeks."

- **Why this matters for you (developer/entrepreneur):** The wide pricing spread between tiers signals OpenAI is optimizing explicitly for cost-sensitive, high-volume agentic workloads — worth designing a "cheap-default, escalate-to-flagship-only-when-needed" routing pattern into your API cost model now, ahead of GA.
- **Business/project idea:** Build a **model-routing SDK** that automatically downgrades routine requests to Luna/Terra-tier models and escalates only complex or coding-heavy requests to Sol, cutting customer API spend — pitch it to AI-wrapper startups currently paying flagship-tier prices for everything.

## 4. OpenAI and Broadcom unveil "Jalapeño," a custom AI inference chip cutting costs ~50%

**OpenAI** and **Broadcom** unveiled **Jalapeño**, OpenAI's first custom chip — a reticle-sized **ASIC** built specifically for **LLM inference**, developed in an unusually fast nine-month cycle partly accelerated by using OpenAI's own models in the design process. The companies say it delivers substantially better performance-per-watt than current state-of-the-art hardware and, per Bloomberg, cuts inference costs by roughly **50%**; it leans on Broadcom's Tomahawk networking silicon and is the first step in a multi-generation custom compute platform, targeting deployment in **late 2026**.

- **Why this matters for you (developer/entrepreneur):** A ~50% inference cost reduction at OpenAI's scale will eventually pressure API pricing industry-wide, and signals custom inference silicon (not just Nvidia GPUs) is becoming a real alternative — relevant context if you're weighing API spend versus self-hosting over the next year.
- **Business/project idea:** Build a **buy-vs-build cost benchmarking tool** for high-volume inference workloads that compares Nvidia GPU rental, custom ASICs (Jalapeño, TPU, Trainium), and self-hosted open-weight models, helping startups time their infrastructure migration as new chip options come online.

## 5. Anthropic wins partial U.S. clearance to restore Claude Mythos 5 access for ~100 organizations

**Anthropic** received U.S. government clearance to restore access to **Claude Mythos 5** for roughly **100 cleared companies and federal agencies**, partially reversing a **Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS)** export-control directive that had forced Anthropic to disable both Mythos 5 and **Claude Fable 5** for all customers worldwide on June 12, over concerns the models could be jailbroken for cybersecurity tasks like vulnerability discovery. Foreign nationals at the now-cleared organizations are exempt from individual export licensing for Mythos 5; everyone else still needs a license, and **Fable 5 remains excluded** from the clearance entirely.

- **Why this matters for you (developer/entrepreneur):** Frontier AI models are now formally being treated as dual-use, export-controlled technology — if your team includes foreign nationals or you serve international customers, model access can change overnight regardless of your contract, so build vendor/model redundancy into your architecture now.
- **Business/project idea:** A **compliance-aware AI gateway** that tracks which frontier models are under export restriction in real time and automatically routes requests to a compliant model or region for regulated/multinational customers would be a timely product given how often this has shifted in the past month.

## 6. Google, Microsoft, GitHub, Nvidia and others launch the Agentic Resource Discovery (ARD) spec

**Google**, with **Microsoft, GitHub, Nvidia, Hugging Face, Salesforce, Snowflake, Cisco, Databricks, ServiceNow, and GoDaddy**, published the **Agentic Resource Discovery (ARD)** specification — an open, **Apache 2.0**-licensed protocol letting AI agents find, evaluate, and verify tools, APIs, and other agents at runtime, via a static `ai-catalog.json` manifest plus a federated registry API. Building on it, **GitHub** is launching **"Agent Finder,"** letting GitHub Copilot dynamically discover and call the right **MCP servers**, skills, and tools for a given task, with guides already covering Claude, ChatGPT, Copilot, and Gemini as ARD clients.

- **Why this matters for you (developer/entrepreneur):** This is shaping up to be foundational interoperability infrastructure for the agentic web, similar in spirit to how robots.txt or OpenAPI became default plumbing — adopting it early could make your tools/APIs discoverable by every major AI agent ecosystem at once instead of integrating with each one separately.
- **Business/project idea:** Publish an `ai-catalog.json` for your product or API now, and offer an **"ARD readiness" audit service** to other startups — being an early, well-documented ARD-compliant tool puts you ahead once Copilot, Gemini, and Claude agents default to discovering tools this way.
- Sources: [Google Developers Blog](https://developers.googleblog.com/announcing-the-agentic-resource-discovery-specification/) · [Microsoft Command Line](https://commandline.microsoft.com/agentic-resource-discovery-specification-ard/) · [Search Engine Journal](https://www.searchenginejournal.com/google-microsoft-back-draft-ai-agent-discovery-spec/579894/) · [Hugging Face](https://huggingface.co/blog/agentic-resource-discovery-launch)

## 7. "Agentjacking": MCP security flaw hijacks Claude Code, Cursor, and Codex via fake Sentry alerts

Security firm **Tenet Security** disclosed **"agentjacking,"** an attack class where a single forged, unauthenticated error event sent to an exposed **Sentry DSN** gets relayed via Sentry's **MCP server** to AI coding agents — including **Claude Code, Cursor, and OpenAI Codex CLI** — which fail to distinguish externally-sourced data from trusted instructions and execute attacker-controlled commands with the developer's own OS privileges. Tenet found **2,388 organizations** with exposed, injectable Sentry credentials and measured an **85% success rate** in controlled tests, with no alerts firing in EDR, IAM, or firewalls; the **Cloud Security Alliance** has classified it as a systemic **MCP vulnerability class**, and VentureBeat reports the same blind spot affects **Datadog, PagerDuty, and Jira** integrations too.

- **Why this matters for you (developer/entrepreneur):** If your team has any MCP-connected observability or ticketing tool (Sentry, Datadog, PagerDuty, Jira) wired into a coding agent that can run shell commands, you likely have an unmonitored remote-code-execution path right now — this needs an immediate audit, not a "someday" fix.
- **Business/project idea:** Build an **"MCP trust boundary" gateway** that sits between coding agents and MCP servers, sanitizing or flagging instruction-like content in tool outputs before the agent acts on it — sell it to engineering orgs scaling up agentic coding tool adoption, where this exact gap is now public knowledge.
- Sources: [The Hacker News](https://thehackernews.com/2026/06/agentjacking-attack-tricks-ai-coding.html) · [VentureBeat](https://venturebeat.com/security/the-attack-that-hijacked-claude-code-came-through-sentry-datadog-pagerduty-and-jira-have-the-same-exposure) · [The New Stack](https://thenewstack.io/agentjacking-sentry-mcp-attack/) · [Tenet Security](https://tenetsecurity.ai/blog/agentjacking-coding-agents-with-fake-sentry-errors/)

## 8. Nvidia's China AI-chip market share projected to collapse from ~40% to ~8% as Huawei surges to ~50%

A **Bernstein** equity research note, reported by the **Associated Press**, finds **Nvidia's** share of China's AI chip market — roughly matched with **Huawei** at ~40% each in 2025 — is projected to **fall to about 8% this year**, while Huawei's grows to roughly **50%**, driven by U.S. export controls and the fact that Huawei's **Ascend 950** chips are now seen as broadly comparable to Nvidia's **H200**.

- **Why this matters for you (developer/entrepreneur):** If you have any China-facing AI product plans, design around a Huawei/Ascend-centric stack rather than CUDA/Nvidia from the outset; conversely, this frees up Nvidia capacity for non-China customers, which may modestly ease global GPU allocation pressure elsewhere.
- **Business/project idea:** A **cross-stack inference compatibility layer** that translates CUDA-based inference pipelines to run on Ascend and other non-Nvidia silicon would be valuable for any vendor needing to serve both U.S. and China-based deployments from a single codebase.
